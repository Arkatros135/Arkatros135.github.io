N5E

- A Naruto Tabletop System, Inspired by D&D 5th edition

What is N5E

Using D&D as a base structure, N5E or Naruto 5E or 5th edition is a newer Tabletop RPG. Using the bare-bones mechanics from Dungeons and Dragons, another tabletop that has been well established since 2014 with its 5th edition. At its core, tabletop RPGs are collaborative storytelling experiences—essentially “adult pretend” with a structured rule set to add balance and unpredictability. D&D, a well-established game since the release of its original 5th Edition in 2014, has seen five major iterations, not counting the ".5" editions like 3.5. While widely popular, the system has faced criticism over the years, whether for limited customization, rising costs, or controversies like the OGL (Open Gaming License) incident involving its publisher, Wizards of the Coast.

Despite these challenges, D&D 5e remains a cornerstone of the TTRPG community, supported by an abundance of homebrew tools and third-party content. Its flexible mechanics inspired many smaller TTRPGs to adopt its framework and build new systems. Some of these games stayed closely aligned with D&D mechanics, offering 5e compatibility as homebrew or supplementary content. Others, like N5E, used D&D 5e as a foundation but expanded in new directions, creating systems that are distinct yet familiar.

N5E, created by a developer known as “King” in 2020, is one such system. It incorporates entirely new mechanics designed to align with the Naruto universe’s themes and powers. Naruto, a massively popular anime and manga, is part of the “Big 3,” a trio of iconic series that defined the golden age of anime. Alongside One Piece and Bleach, Naruto captured audiences worldwide with its themes of perseverance, bonds, and personal growth.

While the N5E system initially focused solely on Naruto, the game has since expanded to include resources for other genres and series. The N5E community, which currently has close to 3,000 members, actively creates homebrew content that enriches the system. This includes mechanics, classes, and settings inspired by various anime, shows, and even fables. The growing library of tools and options benefits both players and game masters, ensuring the system remains engaging and adaptable. Resources now extend beyond Naruto, incorporating elements from the rest of the Big 3 and other franchises. The result is a versatile system that can cater to a wide variety of stories and settings, making N5E a unique addition to the TTRPG landscape.

TTRPGs

Tabletop Role-Playing Games (TTRPGs) is the acronym for Tabletop Role-Playing Games. As nerdy as it might sound, it’s all in good fun! Unlike typical RPGs (Role-Playing Games) such as Skyrim, Fallout, or Diablo, TTRPGs incorporate a "tabletop" aspect where players interact with each other in a shared storytelling experience. Instead of one or a few players using screens, players come together—often physically but increasingly online thanks to digital tools—and immerse themselves in a collaborative story. These games are guided by a GM (Game Master) or created collectively, with the narrative shaped by players’ unpredictable decisions.

TTRPGs originally evolved from simpler strategy games like chess, shogi, and early war games. The addition of imagination and variability gave rise to a more dynamic form of entertainment. This evolution emphasized creativity and autonomy, allowing players to explore unique, unscripted scenarios beyond traditional rule-bound gameplay.

The appeal of TTRPGs lies in their versatility and inclusiveness. Players can create and embody unique characters, form rich stories, and engage in cooperative problem-solving. Dice rolls and other mechanics add elements of chance, enhancing the excitement while maintaining player agency.

The genre gained traction in the late 1970s, with the release of Dungeons & Dragons (D&D), which became a cultural phenomenon by the 1980s and 90s. Since then, the hobby has thrived, fueled by new editions and a diverse array of systems catering to different playstyles. The main popular ones are, D&D, Pathfinder, Call of Cthulhu, Monsters and Masterminds, Blades in the Dark, and Warhammer 40k.

In recent years, digital tools like Roll20, Foundry VTT, and D&D Beyond have revolutionized how TTRPGs are played. These platforms make it easier for people across the globe to connect and play together, sometimes with no physical materials needed. Streams and podcasts such as Critical Role, Dimension 20, and The Adventure Zone have also propelled TTRPGs into mainstream culture, inspiring new players and game creators.

TTRPGs now offer unprecedented diversity in genres, settings, and systems, from traditional fantasy to sci-fi, horror, and even comedy-focused games. Many indie titles explore niche themes or offer rules-light mechanics for easier access. Some systems like Powered by the Apocalypse (PbtA) and Fate are designed for narrative-driven storytelling, minimizing complex rules in favor of creative freedom.

While I’ve often heard people say they might enjoy trying TTRPGs, many don’t because of time constraints or other reasons. Still, I highly recommend it as an experience for everyone, especially if they can find a game that matches their interests. Unfortunately, I know many people who were turned away from the community after a game or experience that didn’t fit their preferences—whether it was the setting, style of play, or group dynamics.

I believe there’s a TTRPG out there for everyone, but not everyone has the immediate know-how or the motivation to explore what they truly want from a game. TTRPGs shouldn’t be viewed as a one-time experience; they deserve a second chance if the first try didn’t quite click. In my time playing, I’ve learned that it won’t always work out, but that’s all the more reason to keep searching for a game or group that does.
